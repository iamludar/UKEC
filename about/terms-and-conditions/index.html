‍13. Plagiarism
‍Academic dishonesty covers any attempt by a student to gain unfair advantage (e.g. extra marks) for her/himself, or for other students, by unauthorised means. Examples of such dishonesty include collusion, deceit, plagiarism and cheating in examinations.Collusion includes, but not limited to, the conscious collaboration, without official approval, between two or more students, in the preparation and production of work which is then submitted as individual work. In cases where one (or more) student has copied from another, all students involved may be penalised.Deceit includes misrepresentation or non-disclosure of relevant information, including the failure to disclose any cases of work being submitted for assessment which has been or will be used for other academic purposes.Plagiarism is the act of using other people’s words, images etc. as if they were your own. In order to make clear to readers the distinction between your words, images etc.Cheating is defined as any attempt to gain an unfair advantage in an assessment (including examinations), or assisting another student to do so. It includes: copying from other candidates, collusion, impersonation, plagiarism, and unauthorised access to unseen examination papers.It is in the best interests of all students for  London Institute of Business Technology to maintain the good reputation of its awards. Your cooperation is expected in actively protecting the integrity of the assessment process. It is the duty of all students to the London Institute of Business Technology to have high personal standards of academic honesty in their studies and to report any instances of malpractice of which they become aware.

Academic dishonesty constitutes any endeavours by a student to secure unfair advantage (for example, additional marks) for themselves or for fellow students through unauthorised means. The London Institute of Business Technology elucidates that examples of such dishonesty encompass collusion, deceit, plagiarism, and cheating in examinations, all of which fall under the purview of our comprehensive Malpractice Policy and Procedure.

Collusion is delineated as the deliberate collaboration, absent of official authorisation, between two or more students in the preparation and production of work which is subsequently submitted as individual effort. In instances where copying from one student by another is identified, it is conceivable that penalties may be imposed on all students implicated in the process.

Deceit refers to either the misrepresentation or non-disclosure of pertinent information. This includes the failure to divulge instances where work submitted for assessment has been or is planned to be used for alternative academic purposes.

Plagiarism, which is explicitly covered under the Malpractice Policy and Procedure, is defined as the appropriation of another individual's words, images, or ideas, presenting them as one's own without proper acknowledgment or citation. It is imperative for students to delineate clearly for readers the demarcation between their original contributions and the work of others.

Cheating encompasses any attempts to gain unfair advantage in an assessment (including examinations), or facilitating another student in doing so. This involves, but is not limited to: copying from fellow candidates, engaging in collusion, impersonating others, committing plagiarism, and gaining unauthorised access to examination papers prior to the examination.

The London Institute of Business Technology has a vested interest in preserving the esteemed reputation of its awards. Therefore, students are expected to cooperate actively in safeguarding the integrity of the assessment process. It is incumbent upon all students affiliated with the London Institute of Business Technology to uphold rigorous standards of academic honesty in their studies and to promptly report any observed instances of malpractice. Your adherence to these principles of academic integrity is obligatory and highly valued.
